Publication

Designing Loot Boxes: Implications for Profits and Welfare

Jin Miao, Sanjay Jain

forthcoming in Marketing Science

Read the Full Paper Online

A loot box is a probabilistic allocation of virtual products, the exact outcome of which is known to consumers only after purchase. Consumers sometimes purchase these goods multiple times until their preferred products are obtained. As loot boxes have been gaining enormous popularity in recent years, they are often criticized as exploitative and socially wasteful. In this study, we develop a stylized model to study the optimal design of loot boxes and its impact on profits and social welfare. We find that firms may assign asymmetric probabilities to ex ante symmetric products. Firms could use loot boxes to offer products at low prices to users who would not buy these products under the traditional pricing strategy. Loot boxes enable firms to earn higher profits due to better price discrimination and market expansion. Contrary to the widespread criticism of loot boxes as socially harmful, our analysis reveals that the loot box strategy can improve social welfare. Some platforms promise that consumers can obtain their preferred products with no more than a certain number of purchases. Contrary to conventional wisdom, our analysis reveals that such a strategy can increase firm’s profits while reducing consumer welfare.

Working Paper

Pricing of Services: An Analysis of the Impact of Availability Bias

Sanjay Jain, Jin Miao

Firms often offer subscriptions for services such as extended warranties for automobiles or appliances, as well as subscription plans for golf clubs or symphony tickets. Some sellers provide exclusive services to subscribers, while others allow non-subscribers to request services. Compared to the service price for non-subscribers, some sellers offer subscribers free services, while others charge discounted rates. In addition, reputable sources like Consumer Reports have criticized some extended warranties as overpriced and exploitative. From the consumers’ perspective, before purchasing a subscription, they need to estimate the probability of needing the service in the future. As prior research has shown that consumers often make errors in predicting future needs, we draw on the literature on availability bias (Tversky and Kahneman 1973) to model how consumers form subjective probabilities of needing the service. We then examine the optimal design of service contracts when the seller serves consumers influenced by availability heuristics. We find that availability bias can sometimes benefit consumers at the expense of firms. We establish conditions under which a firm will offer subscribers free services vis-a-vis a discounted rate. When firms can offer subscription plans, they tend to set higher spot market prices. Our results demonstrate that availability bias can lead firms to provide socially wasteful services, where the cost of service exceeds the valuation for all consumers. Furthermore, we show that availability bias leads firms to bundle exclusive services with certain subscription plans. Therefore, our model offers one potential explanation for the variety of service contracts on the market. Even in a perfectly competitive market, our findings indicate that social welfare is lower due to availability bias.
